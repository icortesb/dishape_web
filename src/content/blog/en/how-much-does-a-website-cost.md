"How much does a website cost?" is the first question on almost every project, and the honest answer is that there's no single number. A website is like a building: a shed and a three-story house don't cost the same, even though both are "a building". What follows is how to understand where the budget actually goes, so you can compare proposals without the price being a black box.

## Why there's no fixed price

A "website" can be anything between a single-screen site with a contact form and an application with user accounts, payments and an admin panel. Between those two extremes there's a huge difference in work, which is why anyone who throws out a number before understanding what's needed is guessing.

The price is defined by **what the site has to do**, not by the number of screens. A single screen that processes payments and connects to a billing system is more complex than ten screens of static content.

## What pushes the budget up or down

These are the factors that move a project's cost the most:

- **Custom design vs. template.** A template is cheap and fast, but it shows and looks like thousands of other sites. A custom design costs more because it's real design work, built for a specific business.
- **Functionality.** An informational site is at one end. Adding an online store, bookings, user accounts, payment or CRM integrations moves the budget up at every step.
- **Content.** If the copy and images already exist, it saves time. If they have to be created, that's part of the work.
- **Integrations.** Connecting the site to external systems (payment methods, invoicing, tools the business already uses) adds development.
- **Performance and technical SEO.** A site that loads fast and is ready for Google isn't automatic: it's work done well from the start, or paid for dearly fixing it later.

## The cost almost nobody counts: coordinating vendors

There's a cost that doesn't appear in any quote but weighs heavily: coordination. When one person does the design, another the development, a third the server, and an agency ties it together, each one adds their markup and the client ends up as the go-between for everyone. The project stalls at every handoff and no one is accountable for the whole.

Having a single person in charge of every piece doesn't just lower the direct cost: it removes that invisible overhead of meetings, waiting and miscommunication between teams.

## How to think about your budget

Instead of starting with "how much does it cost", it's better to start with **what result you're after**. A site meant to generate inquiries isn't the same investment as one meant to sell products online, and the two are justified by different numbers.

A good proposal should include a clear scope, deliverables, timelines and a **fixed price agreed before starting**, not an open invoice that grows along the way. If a quote doesn't explain where the money goes, that's information in itself.

## In short

A website's price depends on what it has to solve, not on a fixed catalog. Cheap turns expensive when it has to be redone, and expensive without judgment doesn't guarantee results either. What matters is understanding the scope, having a single point of accountability and a price agreed up front.

For a real number on a specific case, the fastest path is to describe what's needed and get a clear proposal. That costs nothing and clears up the biggest unknown of the project.
