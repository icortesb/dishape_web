Since generative AI became accessible, "add a chatbot" turned into the default answer for any business. But an AI chatbot isn't good or bad in itself: it's a tool that solves concrete problems and creates new ones when used where it doesn't belong. Here's how to tell whether it makes sense for your case.

## What an AI chatbot does well

An AI assistant, trained on the business's information, shines in concrete situations:

- **After-hours service.** It answers questions at any time, so an opportunity isn't lost because it was 11 p.m.
- **Repetitive questions.** The same recurring doubts (hours, prices, how to buy, order status) get answered instantly and free up people's time.
- **First filter.** It handles the initial query, gathers the details and, when a person is needed, hands the case over with the context already assembled.
- **Scales without added cost.** Ten queries or a thousand, the cost of answering doesn't spike with volume.

The common thread: these are high-volume, low-complexity tasks, where speed matters more than nuance.

## When a chatbot gets in the way

There are also cases where a bot subtracts instead of adds:

- **Complex or consultative sales.** If the sale needs to understand a particular problem and build trust, sending the person to a generic bot pushes them away rather than closer.
- **Sensitive matters.** Complaints, delicate issues or situations where the customer is already upset: there they want a person, not an automated menu.
- **When there's no good information to feed it.** A chatbot is only as good as the information it's trained on. If that base is poor or outdated, the bot makes things up or answers badly, and that's worse than not having it.

## What separates a useful chatbot from an annoying one

The difference isn't in the technology, it's in how it's implemented. A good assistant:

1. **Is trained on the business's real information**, not generic answers anyone could give.
2. **Knows when it doesn't know** and hands off to a person instead of making things up.
3. **Answers in the business's tone**, not like an impersonal robot.
4. **Gets measured.** It records what users ask, to improve service with data instead of guesses.

A canned bot that answers anything just to answer creates more frustration than it saves. A well-built one feels like an extension of the business.

## Beyond chat: agents and automation

The chatbot is the visible face, but AI applied to a business can go beyond answering. Wired to the tools and data already in use, it can carry out actions: place an order, schedule, move information between systems. That's where it stops being "a chat" and becomes a process that runs on its own.

## In short

An AI chatbot is worth it when there's a volume of repetitive queries and it's trained on good information, with the ability to hand off to a person when needed. It's not worth it as a replacement for service that requires human judgment. The right question isn't "should I add a chatbot?", but "what concrete problem do I want to solve?". With that answer clear, deciding whether AI is the right tool is simple.
